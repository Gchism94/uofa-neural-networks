Key Concepts ▾
Why SGD zigzags: Vanilla SGD updates each parameter by the same learning rate regardless of gradient history. In narrow valleys the gradient oscillates between walls, causing slow, zigzagging descent toward the minimum.
What Momentum adds: Momentum accumulates a velocity vector — a weighted average of past gradients. It accelerates in consistent directions and dampens oscillations, letting the optimizer "roll through" small bumps and converge faster.
How AdaGrad adapts: AdaGrad divides each gradient by the square root of its accumulated squared history. Parameters that receive large, frequent gradients get smaller effective learning rates — useful for sparse data but G grows without bound, eventually freezing learning.
Why RMSProp fixes AdaGrad: RMSProp replaces AdaGrad's running sum with an exponentially decaying average (controlled by ρ). This prevents the learning rate from decaying to zero, keeping the optimizer effective deep into training.
What Adam combines: Adam maintains both a first moment (momentum, like Momentum SGD) and a second moment (variance, like RMSProp), with bias-correction in early steps. It tends to converge quickly and works well across a wide range of architectures and learning rates.
