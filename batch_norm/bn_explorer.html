Key Concepts ▾
Internal covariate shift: as a network trains, earlier layers' parameters change — shifting the input distribution to each subsequent layer. Deeper layers must constantly adapt, slowing convergence and requiring careful initialization.
Normalization step: for each mini-batch, BN computes the mean and variance of each feature, then normalizes to zero mean and unit variance. This keeps the input distribution to each layer stable regardless of upstream parameter changes.
γ and β (scale and shift): learnable parameters that follow normalization. Without them, BN would force all activations to N(0,1), losing representational power. γ and β let the network learn the optimal scale and mean for each feature — including recovering the identity if that's best.
Higher learning rates: normalized inputs reduce sensitivity to weight scale and initialization. Networks with BN can be trained with 5–10× higher learning rates without diverging — the stabilized distributions prevent gradient explosion and vanishing.
Training vs. inference: during training, BN normalizes using the current mini-batch's mean and variance. During inference, it uses exponential moving averages of those statistics accumulated over training — so batch size doesn't affect inference.

The Four Batch Normalization Operations
Step 1
Compute Batch Mean
μ_B = (1/m) Σ x_i
Average activation across all m samples in the mini-batch for each feature dimension.
Step 2
Compute Variance
σ²_B = (1/m) Σ (x_i − μ_B)²
Measure spread after centering. Captures how much activations deviate from the mean.
Step 3
Normalize
x̂_i = (x_i − μ_B) / √(σ²_B + ε)
Divide by standard deviation (with ε for numerical stability). Result has zero mean and unit variance.
Step 4
Scale & Shift
y_i = γ · x̂_i + β
Learnable γ and β restore representational power — the network can un-normalize if that's optimal.
